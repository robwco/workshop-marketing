Why do dry spells happen?

If you’re a freelancer or consultant you’ve probably noticed your business is plagued by a vicious feast or famine business cycle. This means that all of the time you have to look over your shoulder.

Every day you live in fear that an untimely bill will put your checking account over the edge and in the red.

You know that dry spells poke holes in your income, business trajectory, and sanity. Yet you probably don’t know the true cause of them.

It’s not why you think. Dry spells are NOT a natural part of freelancing.

Not entirely anyway.

While it’s true there are natural ebbs and flows to any sales cycle – long periods without work don’t occur just because.

They happen because of specific actions you take (or don’t).

Think back to the last time you were stressed out about not having any work. It probably kicked your butt into high gear. In fact, that’s probably the last time you dedicated whole days to looking for work.

It made you desperate and you probably hated that feeling… but guess what? It also probably worked. After all, you made it out.

Have you ever gone through a rough patch only to later have way more clients than you can handle come to you for work all at once?

It’s easy to throw your hands up and curse: “Oh sure NOW everyone wants to hire me. Where were all of these clients 2 weeks ago?”

What you don’t realize, though, is you caused it. If you hadn’t waited until you had no work to start a mad dash of client outreach, you could have totally avoided this situation altogether.

The truth is dry spells actually begin when you’re fully-booked!

That’s why the trick is to not wait until you’re out of work to do something about them.

It sounds easy, so why don’t freelancers do it? Because when you have work you’re too busy to look for leads. It’s that simple.

Now you might be thinking: sure this all makes sense but if I’m too busy, then I’m too busy – and forcing myself to keep up with an endless treadmill of sales activity doesn’t sound like something I want any part of.

That’s why you need to break your freelance sales cycle into a set of small actions you can do in 15-20 minutes every day.

See, just like client projects tend to go way over the timeline you have in your head at the beginning of a project, the income you get from them won’t sustain you as long as you think it will.

Before you know it you’ll be out of money, and still have to finish up the project.

That results in a huge loss all around.

Worse, relying on sheer willpower to create sales won’t work. That’s why your inbox is distraction central instead of a sales machine.

You need a system that you can follow effortlessly, no matter how busy you are.
